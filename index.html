Introduction

In the quiet twilight of memory, where the essence of a person’s life - their joys, sorrows, and triumphs - slowly dims. It’s a realm where the familiar contours of identity are relentlessly eroded, not by the passage of time, but by an insidious ailment that disassembles the core of who they are. Each forgotten name, each lost memory, is a star winking out in the galaxy of a person’s life story, leaving behind a haunting void where once there was a rich tapestry of experiences and connections.

Quick Facts

Prevalence

More than 6 million Americans are living with Alzheimer's. 
By 2050, this number is projected to rise to 13m.

Mortality

1 in 3 seniors dies with Alzheimer's or another dementia. It kills more than breast and prostate cancer combined.

Econ Burden

In 2023, Alzheimer’s and other dementias cost the nation $345 billion. By 2050, these costs could rise to $1 trillion.

To date, there hasn’t been a new drug approved for Alzheimer’s disease in 20 years. 2023 marks a pivotal year in Alzheimer’s research with the breakthrough of two promising drugs, Lecanemab and Donanemab.

Motivation

Our project is driven by the desire to deeply understand Alzheimer's disease in its entirety - its distribution, associated factors, and the transformative potential of these new drugs. We aim to explore how these advancements might alter the course of Alzheimer's disease.

Associated Factors

Health Status

Research has revealed that non-genetic risk factors for Alzheimer's disease include various aspects of health status, such as age, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, and a history of head trauma, etc. 

Socioeconomic status

Socioeconomic factors, including income, expenditure, etc. can significantly influence lifestyle choices and the accessibility of healthcare resources, which in turn may contribute to the risk and progression of Alzheimer's disease.

Personal characteristics

Personal characteristics, such as sex, race, education level, etc. are also known to influence the risk and progression of Alzheimer's disease.

New Hope

Lecanemab

Treatment with LEQEMBI (Lecanemab) was proven to slow the progression of early Alzheimer's disease, helping people remember, solve problems, and complete daily activities for longer.
